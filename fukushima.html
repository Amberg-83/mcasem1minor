Fukushima Daiichi Nuclear Accident (2011)

Cause of Disaster

The Fukushima Daiichi nuclear accident occurred on March 11, 2011, after a massive 9.0 magnitude earthquake struck the northeastern coast of Japan. The earthquake triggered a powerful tsunami with waves reaching up to 15 meters high, overwhelming the plant’s seawall and flooding the emergency generators. As a result, the cooling systems failed, leading to overheating and the meltdown of three reactors.

The loss of electrical power—both external grid power and internal backup generators— caused the nuclear fuel rods to heat uncontrollably. Hydrogen gas built up inside the reactor units, resulting in a series of explosions that damaged the reactor buildings. The failure to maintain cooling allowed radioactive materials to escape into the atmosphere and surrounding seawater, creating long-term contamination.

Although the plant workers, known as the “Fukushima 50,” worked tirelessly to contain the situation, the event became one of the worst nuclear disasters in history. The Japanese government declared a nuclear emergency and evacuated more than 160,000 people from the region, many of whom could not return even years later due to radioactive contamination.

Deaths

Immediate radiation deaths: 0 Indirect disaster-related deaths (evacuation stress, delayed medical care): 1,000+ Long-term radiation-related deaths remain under study.

Major Pollutants Released

  • Cesium-134
  • Cesium-137
  • Iodine-131
  • Strontium-90 (trace amounts)
