Overview & Context

Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster occurred on March 11, 2011, following a powerful earthquake and tsunami. Backup power systems failed, leading to overheating, hydrogen explosions, and large-scale radioactive release.

Causes of the Disaster

The primary cause was a massive earthquake and tsunami that exceeded the plant’s design limits. Flooding disabled backup generators, stopping all cooling systems. Inadequate disaster preparedness and insufficient protection against extreme natural hazards worsened the impact.

Technical Failure Mechanism

Loss of electrical power prevented coolant circulation. Fuel rods overheated, generating hydrogen gas. Hydrogen explosions damaged reactor buildings, releasing radioactive materials into the environment.

Short-Term and Long-Term Impacts

Short-term effects included evacuation of over 150,000 people and radiation exposure risks. Long-term impacts include environmental contamination, economic losses, psychological trauma, and decades-long decommissioning efforts.

Lessons Learned

The disaster emphasized the need for strong safety culture, improved disaster risk assessment, backup power redundancy, and transparent emergency response systems.
