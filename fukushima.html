Fukushima Daiichi Nuclear Disaster (2011)

Background

The Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster occurred on March 11, 2011, after a powerful 9.0 magnitude earthquake struck Japan. The earthquake triggered a massive tsunami that overwhelmed the coastal defenses of the nuclear power plant, cutting off power and disabling critical cooling systems in three reactors. This led to core meltdowns, hydrogen explosions, and the release of radioactive material into the air and sea.

The disaster was the largest nuclear crisis since Chernobyl and exposed weaknesses in nuclear plant design, emergency planning, and tsunami protection. Japan was forced to evacuate more than 150,000 residents from the surrounding areas.

Fukushima also had long-lasting social and economic impacts. Thousands of residents lost their homes, farmlands were contaminated, and fishing industries suffered due to radioactive ocean discharge. Cleanup and decommissioning efforts are still ongoing and are expected to continue for decades.

Cause of Disaster

Although the reactors automatically shut down during the earthquake, the real problem began when the tsunami flooded the plant. Backup generators located in low-lying areas were destroyed, leaving no electricity to run the cooling pumps. Without cooling, the reactor cores overheated and melted down.

Multiple hydrogen explosions damaged the outer buildings, spreading radioactive dust into the atmosphere. Unlike Chernobyl, the containment structures remained mostly intact, preventing a larger release, but the damage was still significant.

Deaths and Health Effects

There were no immediate radiation deaths. However, over 1,000 people died indirectly due to evacuation stress, disruption of medical treatment, and long-term psychological trauma. Radiation exposure among the public remained relatively low due to rapid evacuation.

Major Pollutants Released

  • Cesium-134
  • Cesium-137
  • Iodine-131
  • Contaminated seawater (treated but released over years)
