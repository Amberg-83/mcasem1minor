Overview & Context

The Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster occurred on March 11, 2011, after one of the most powerful earthquakes ever recorded struck off Japan’s northeastern coast. The resulting tsunami overwhelmed the plant’s seawall, flooding backup generators and disabling the cooling systems. Although the reactors shut down automatically during the earthquake, the loss of power prevented circulation of coolant water, leading to dangerous overheating of the fuel rods. Within hours, the situation escalated, and over the next few days, three reactors experienced partial meltdowns. The hydrogen produced by the overheated fuel caused large explosions that severely damaged the facility, releasing radioactive materials into the air and ocean.

Japanese authorities quickly ordered mass evacuations from nearby towns and villages, ultimately displacing more than 150,000 people. Many were forced to leave behind belongings, homes, and family property without knowing whether they would ever return. Although radiation exposure levels were significantly lower than those in Chernobyl, the disaster created widespread psychological and social distress.

Environmental impacts of the accident remain a concern. Large amounts of radioactive contamination entered local soil and water bodies, prompting one of the largest decontamination efforts in Japan’s history. The handling of radioactive wastewater continues to spark global debate.

More than a decade later, the Fukushima site remains in long-term decommissioning. The disaster reshaped global nuclear safety policies.
