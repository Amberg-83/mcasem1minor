Chernobyl Nuclear Disaster (1986)

Cause of Disaster

The Chernobyl disaster occurred on April 26, 1986, when Reactor 4 at the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant in northern Ukraine exploded during a late-night safety test. A combination of design flaws in the RBMK reactor and critical operator mistakes led to an uncontrollable surge of energy, causing a massive steam explosion followed by a nuclear fire that burned for 10 days.

The explosion ejected radioactive material into the atmosphere, contaminating large parts of Ukraine, Belarus, and Russia. The Soviet Union initially attempted to hide the disaster, but radiation sensors in Sweden detected abnormal levels, forcing the government to acknowledge the accident publicly. The lack of immediate evacuation contributed to large-scale radiation exposure.

Over 600,000 workers, known as “liquidators,” were involved in cleaning the surrounding area and constructing the original concrete sarcophagus around the reactor. The entire city of Pripyat was abandoned, creating one of the world’s most famous ghost towns. The disaster is considered the worst nuclear accident in history, classified as Level 7 on the INES scale.

Deaths

Immediate deaths: 31 Estimated long-term deaths (cancer): 4,000 to 30,000 Affected population: over 5 million people across Europe

Major Pollutants Released

  • Iodine-131
  • Cesium-137
  • Strontium-90
  • Plutonium isotopes
