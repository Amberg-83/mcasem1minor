Overview & Context

The Chernobyl disaster of April 26, 1986, is widely regarded as the worst nuclear accident in world history. It occurred during a late-night safety test at Reactor No. 4 of the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant in Ukraine, which was then part of the Soviet Union. Due to a flawed reactor design and a series of operational mistakes, operators disabled critical safety systems, causing a sudden and uncontrollable power surge. This led to a massive steam explosion that destroyed the reactor core and blew off the thousand-ton concrete lid.

First responders, including firefighters and plant workers, were exposed to lethal doses of radiation within minutes. The nearby town of Pripyat continued normal life for an entire day because authorities delayed evacuation.

Environmental and long-term health impacts were severe. Radioactive contamination spread across large parts of Europe, and thousands of thyroid cancer cases were linked to radioactive iodine exposure.

Chernobyl became a global symbol of nuclear risk, leading to international safety reforms and the construction of the New Safe Confinement structure completed in 2016.
